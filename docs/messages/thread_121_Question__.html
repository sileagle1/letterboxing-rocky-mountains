My understanding is that the National Park Service is totally
against any objects (letterboxes, geocaches, etc.) being placed
in their parks. If they find one, they will confiscate it and
probably throw it away. They have even been known to look on
the letterboxing.org website to see if any boxes are on their
land, then use the clues to find and confiscate them. It is a
shame since there are many great places to hide boxes on NPS
land, but it is not worth the hassel.
Some State Parks have strict rules, too (my box in Arkansas
was temporarily confiscated), so you may want to ask permission
first.

From: (cadenza74@earthlink.net) | Date: 2003-11-04 22:14:38 UTC-07:00
um, you might be wise to realize that the restrictions with the National
Park system are more than just the fact that they don't like it or frown on
things being left behind. It is illegal to leave things behind in a
National Park. There are a few people that have snuck a few in under the
table, but many of those have been confiscated and they certainly aren't
listed on the LBNA site since that could get the site it trouble.

I have always been sad about these policies. Since I live right by
Yellowstone and the Grand Tetons, I always want to plant there, but I have
to resist the urge. :-)

From: rvn_wolf (treikya@aol.com) | Date: 2003-11-06 00:00:44 UTC
Hello all.

I actually wrote to the Colorado State Parks to see what their policy
was. This is what they wrote back:

>Thank you for your inquiry into Colorado state parks' policy
>concerning
>geocaching and letterboxing. As far as I am aware, there are no
>regulations with respect to these hobbies. However, I am certain
>that these
>activities would be allowed in our parks provided that the animals
>and
>environment would not experience a disturbance.

>If you have additional questions, or if I can be of any more
>assistance,
>please feel free to contact me. Thank you.

>Cherylynne Gerton
>Administrative Assistant III
>Colorado State Parks


So, it sounds like as long as you don't have people tromping waaayyy
off trails, and pestering the wildlife it should be okay. Or at
least, you can tell the people at the parks that Cerylynne Gerton
said it was. ;]
