OK Guys
I understand not wanting people to take our boxes, but what if there is
somebody out there just waiting for this? What if there is another great
LB'er (we all know who they are here.) just looking for an outlet for
their creative energy?

I live in Fort Collins and would be happy to speak to the writer. But
maybe you wouldn't want me to. I would like some feed back on how to
participate in letterboxing without being selfish. Do we be more
selective in who gets access to clues? Do we have some boxes "open to the
public" and others that are "private?" I have only been letterboxing for
a short while but I can't imagine a better hobby. It is family friendly,
low cost, gets you out and about to see things you never would have
before, you meet great people, it is creative/artistic. WOW! Who
wouldn't want to do this?

I've also seen incredible boxes stolen for no reason what so ever. There
was one near my old home in Calif with ties to Star Wars. All 4 (I think
there were 4) were stolen. You don't just happen to stumble upon 4 boxes
planted seperately. Somebody had to have gone online, found the clues and
taken them. Just plain mean.

Yet, how many would be here if we hadn't have found out about it by
reading an article?
Carrie

From: JODIE KERN (jodiemkern@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-09-23 15:12:54 UTC-07:00
I would hate to see letterboxing become some exclusive thing where it seems that there are really snobby people that won't let you into their party. That would suck. I have met so so so many nice, great, fun people through letterboxing so far and hope that it continues. I think that word of mouth is a great way to pass the hobby on, I heard about it through a friend that thought I would think it was really cool (thanks Amy!) and I have passed it on to a very few people that I thought would like it too.

I can't remember who said it (and it sounded much cooler when they said it) but I loved what someone said about planting boxes. They were talking about how when they leave it they pretty much give up ownership of it and keep a hope that it will be there for a long time and get lots of visitors. I try to feel that way when I leave mine. Besides, people that steal or damage the boxes expose themselves to eternally bad karma and will always be searching for their lost left shoe. They will get what's coming to them.

Flying Javelinas--
I think it would be great for you to speak to the writer.
This topic has been worked over pretty well on the big list. My
feeling is that it is better for them to find out from you than third
hand from the Time or CNN pieces. I also think there is some valid
concern that with the first wave of activity after publicity, there
will be some people participating that don't understand the finer
points. When I came upon a box lying exposed next to it's hidey hole
with nothing put back in the bags I realized that there is virtue in
making clues a little harder or the hikes a little longer.

It really is personal choice. Some people can accept the fact
that thier letterboxes may dissapear and others want to take measures
to keep them around a little longer. Let everybody suit themselves as
to how to protect their treasures, but by all means do what you can
to get some of the finer points into that article. We can definitely
still use a few energetic newbies.

just my dos centavos,
y-nought

From: Autumn (Autumn@shadowslight.com) | Date: 2004-09-24 04:48:30 UTC
I'll just pitch in my two cents. Boxes go missing for so many
reasons. My Litha box has been reported missing at LEAST three
times. Found by a wonderful geocacher by accident and replaced in
it's proper spot, etc.

So - if you place a box, you take your chance.
It's a thing out in the world.

The article may be really good for the hobby - and really open some
doors for some new boxers on the Front Range. How many were there
when this list started? A half dozen? A dozen? Look how we've
grown in a year! Wonderful boxes with great stamps. Fun people in
our little neck of the world.

So - I'm thinking - someone just do the interview, and we all hope
for the best. Think positively and all that.

Cheers,
Autumn
