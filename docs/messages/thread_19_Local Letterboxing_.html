From: Eoghan (eoghan@sharawadgi.com) | Date: 2003-07-17 03:31:23 UTC
Hello to all!

Thanks to Autumn for setting up the list.
I went camping last weekend and when I got back there were more than
300 new posts to the main talk list. Overwhelming. It was hard enough
to keep up when there started to be more than 10 or 15 posts a day,
but now it's impossible. So again, thanks. I look forward to hearing
the local perspective from everyone, and having a chance to ask
questions or make comments without getting lost in the raging river
of the big list.

A few comments about the previous posts on the list:

1. I also couldn't find the Litha box on my first try and was 'sure'
it had gone MIA. Now that the clues have been revised I'll give it
another shot. Thanks for the updates.

2. Several people asked about carving stuff; I've been able to find
Speedball Speedycut and lithocutters at Guiry's on S Colorado Blvd.
Several different sizes of the pink stuff as well as the crummy
crumbling garbage and the true linoleum mounted on blocks. They've 4
other locations around Denver and one in Boulder. Other art supply
stores might be more likely than hobby or craft stores to carry these
things also, though I haven't checked Meininger yet. My favorite
carving media was the large size StadtlerMars vinyl erasers, but
they've discontinued them and their small ones have a raised logo on
them rather than a printed one, making them useless for carving.
Crumbs! If anyone finds a stash of the large ones anywhere, please
let me know. I'll buy as many as I can find.

3. A thought about the small number of boxes in our area - being a
lonely letterboxer may be frustrating, but the situation can change
very quickly. Before you know it, we might be buried in boxes. The
traffic on the main list attests to that. Two years ago there were
only a handful of boxes in the Portland, OR area. Now they're awash
with them. Perhaps that's a good thing, but from seeing the same
thing happen with geocaching, my personal thought is that "not
enough" can too quickly become "way too much". I prefer a pace where
you can read every clue posted and decide whether to search for the
box or not, and have a chance to go look for it before another
hundred clues build up in your mailbox. But I also prefer small towns
to large ones, and short work weeks to 60+ hour frantic ones, so my
Thoreau-esque bias is obvious. Hopefully letterboxing around here
will hold at some middle point for awhile, where we all have enough
boxes to keep us busy but not enough to start tripping over them at
every turn on the trail.

4. And a final question - What other similar hobbies are people here
interested in? Are there stamp artists, backpackers, mailart
afficionados, geocachers, scrapbookers, orienteers, or 'altered book'
artists here?

As Fiona mentioned, she and I are both into altered books - and
mailart, too! That's how we found letterboxing to begin with. A
friend who trades mail art with us mentioned it, and from there, we
were hooked. I'm not much of a stamp artist (Dixie's found my boxes,
she can attest!) but I'm getting better - besides, the hike is really
the point (I'm saying that to make myself feel better, eh). I've
found one geocache, based solely on the clues - I don't own a GPS. I
think I'd enjoy it, but I love the treasure hunting aspect of boxing
much better than the actual "treasures" of a geocache.

As for the altered books - it was that hobby that inspired the art
journal that is accompanying the postal letterbox. I figured if Mark
can carve a beautiful stamp, the least I could do was create a
beautiful journal to go with it! :)

Thanks for posing the questions - and for joining! Here's a question
for you, too! I noticed that most (all??) of the boxes you've
planted were in TX. Are you out in CO now, or do you just frequent
the Rockies?
