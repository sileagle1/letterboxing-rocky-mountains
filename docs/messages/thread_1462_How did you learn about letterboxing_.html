From: Autumn (Autumn@shadowslight.com) | Date: 2008-01-22 14:31:40 UTC
What a great question!
I found out about letterboxing through an artists site, where the
discussion of hand-carved rubber stamps was in full swing. That was in
late 2002, and I looked for and found my first box in April of 2003.

That first box was "The Rock", planted on Alcatraz Island, in the San
Francisco Bay. I believe that box is gone, or at least the clues are -
because the Island is actually a national park (and we know that's a no-
no). It was a thrill to be certain, and we were such rookies at it
that we nearly missed the last ferry back to the mainland in our delay
in locating the treasure. I'm pretty sure I posted a photo from that
trip in the photos section. :)

Formed this e-group a few months later because of the dearth of
letterboxes just generally in our area. :) Wow ... what a great five
years it's been.

Cheers,
Autumn

Re: How did you learn about letterboxing?

From: jelybean_lb (jelybean@adelphia.net) | Date: 2008-01-22 19:55:30 UTC
I read about LB'ing in a US Airways in-flight magazine while on a
business trip. This was right after the landmark Smithsonian article
came out about the Dartmoor boxes in the late 90s. At that time,
there were not too many letterboxes in the US (as you might imagine
since the Smithsonian article was kind of the catalyst here). But
surprisingly enough, there were SOME boxes here. I started looking
around where I lived (upstate NY at the time) and sure enough there
were a few boxes within a couple hours drive (maybe a half dozen).
My first finds were by a distinguished gentleman named Cock 'O The
Trail who kind of became my mentor. His early boxes had some fine
clues, innovations like multi-boxes, and some neat little tricks I
won't reveal. Remember, this was pretty early, so some people were
pushing the envelope and expanding the hobby in the US even BEFORE
the Smithsonian article came out. There being little choices of
boxes at the time, some of my earliest finds were by people I have
come to think of as the founding fathers and mothers of letterboxing
in the US, like Wanda (F:15,000 - and that's not a typo) and Pete,
Don and Gwen, Silent Doug. Since then, I have "met" many fine
letterboxers and have really enjoyed seeing the way this hobby (and
the amount of boxes!) has grown in the last ten years or so.

JeLyBean
