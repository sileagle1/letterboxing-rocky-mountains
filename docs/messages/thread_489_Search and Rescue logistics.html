Search and Rescue logistics

From: Renee and Dave (haipnsuch@juno.com) | Date: 2005-01-13 15:25:10 UTC-07:00
I am a search and rescue dog handler with a volunteer SAR team based out
of Boulder County, and I do highly recommend that anyone who wanders off
the beaten path have a card. However, the notion that purchasing the
Department of Local Affairs' (DOLA) SAR card helps to staff SAR teams is
inaccurate. The vast majority of search and rescue personnel are
volunteers. These volunteers donate their time, gas money, and most of
their equipment to the cause. Also, my team has never billed a lost
party for their rescue (maybe the state does?). Most years, but not
always, DOLA gives us a small, but much appreciated grant that we use to
purchase things like radios, pagers, and avalanche beacons. But, it is
far from staffing, equipping, and training. Also, with the SAR card, I
do not believe that helicopter medical evacuations are covered, just the
use of a helicopter for searching.

On another note, I will explain a frustration experienced by volunteer
SAR folks: my team typically gets over 50 calls a year, and about 40 of
those get canceled before we even show up. These are mostly due to poor
communication between the hiker and the person at home who dials 911 when
their partner does not show up when they thought they said they would.
Our pagers start beeping during dinner with our families, we grab our
gear, load the dog into the truck, say goodbye, and drive off for the
trailhead to help someone we assume is in need. When we are about half
way there, the pager beeps again with a cancellation message... the
"overdue hiker" wasn't really lost, just stopped for a piece of pie on
the way home (or something like that). What we tell people before they
go out 'a-wandering' is first to take a hiking partner with them, but to
also have someone else (at home) know where they are going, what
equipment they have with them, plan A and plan B, when they expect to
return, and a well-established time they should call 911 if they have not
returned.

In our mission logbook, we have categories we assign for the missing
persons (e.g., Hiker, Climber, Skier, Boater, Hunter, Bicyclist, etc).
Maybe someday we will add a "Letterboxer" category, but so far we haven't
had any of those (that we know of)... maybe because LBers are usually
equipped with map & compass, and a set of directions.

Es - until now, I have never thought about bringing a piece of pie for a
lost person, just a sloppy dog kiss from my German Shepherd. Another
Catamount Carver humbly adds that she and a friend had been the subjects
of a technical climbing rescue in Arizona ... many years ago (early
80's). Arizona Mountain Rescue was wonderful and while they didn't
provide pie, they kindly skirted round the media leaving her and a friend
totally anonymous and not a penny poorer to boot.

~Yoque of the Catamount Carvers
