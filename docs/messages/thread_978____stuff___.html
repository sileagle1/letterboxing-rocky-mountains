From: jelybean_lb (jelybean@adelphia.net) | Date: 2006-01-08 02:27:37 UTC
Hello boys and girls out there in letterbox land. Got a question
for you. Today, team JeLyBean loaded up the candy wagon and headed
out for a 'boxing extravaganza. We found six today (why, thank you)
but four of the six had ...stuff... in them beyond the standard
stamp and logbook. By ...stuff... I mean the kind of things one
typically finds in a geocache.

(Short aside for those of you unfamiliar with geocaching: it's a lot
like letterboxing except you use GPS coordinates to find the box,
and instead of a stamp, the box is full of ...stuff... You bring
your own ...stuff... and take one of the pieces of ...stuff... out
of the geocache and leave one of your own pieces of ...stuff...
Other than that, it's very similar to letterboxing. Except we are
much more cool. Aside over.)

Anyway, here's the question. If you run into this kind
of ...stuff... what do you do with it? Here's the reason I'm
asking. In my experience, geocachers are not always familiar with
letterboxing and how we play our game. Owing to the relative
imprecision of GPS devices, geocachers often show up in an area and
begin hunting around for a likely spot to hide a geocache. They are
not aided by letterboxing clues like "look under the pile of rocks
at the base of the big tree." Because both letterboxers and
geocachers tend to place their boxes in cool areas, there is often a
letterbox and geocache near each other. Often, geocachers will find
the letterbox by mistake and think it is the geocache they were
searching for. The will then open the box and be immediately
disappointed at the lack of ...stuff... They will fall into one of
two categories: 1) the kind that will look through the journal,
realize the stamp in the box may have something to do with the
stamps in the journal, think that this may not exactly be a
geocache, sign in pen or pencil and leave, or 2) the kind that will
think the stamp is the ...stuff... they were looking for, take the
stamp and leave some other ...stuff...

It's the latter of these that concerns me. On more than one
occasion, I have come to a letterbox with no stamp, but does contain
a plastic alligator or wooden nickle. I then know that a category 2
geocacher has been there. Because of this, I now write on the lid
of all my boxes "This is not a geocache."

Anyway, when I see ...stuff... in a letterbox I get worried that
geocachers are thinking the letterbox is the geocache and it is only
a matter of time until the stamp disappears and is replaced by Mardi
Gras beads.

So, what do you do with the ...stuff... take it out, leave it, or
other?

'Box Happy... and don't take the stamp,
JeLyBean

away. I know because we all looked at it last summer during the

not obvious, that, so far, I haven't had any problems. Of course,
it's under about 5 feet of snow right now, so, well, there you go.

And, having said that.. if any of y'all ever visit one of my boxes
and find .. stuff .. in it.. please feel free to remove it and leave
a note!

From: Judy (jowagnon@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-01-09 00:54:23 UTC
I was a geocacher (gasp!) long before letterboxing became my obsession
of choice. It was actually 'caching that got me into 'boxing. I found
a cache at Pineview Reservoir that had a note in the log book about a
letterbox being very near, so I looked for the LB and found it about 6
feet away. Sure enough it had ...stuff... in it, but the stamp was
also still there. So I took the ...stuff... out of it and put it in
the cache and left a note in the LB that if you're looking for the
cache, keep looking because this isn't it. When I went online to learn
about 'boxing, I looked for that letterbox and couldn't find it. It
hadn't even been listed yet so I was definitely the FTF (boxer, that
is). I emailed the placer what I had done which she said it was OK.
Whose box was it? Cadenza's!

> imprecision of GPS devices, geocachers often show up in an area and
> begin hunting around for a likely spot to hide a geocache. They are
> not aided by letterboxing clues like "look under the pile of rocks
> at the base of the big tree." Because both letterboxers and
> geocachers tend to place their boxes in cool areas, there is often a
> letterbox and geocache near each other. Often, geocachers will find
> the letterbox by mistake and think it is the geocache they were
> searching for. The will then open the box and be immediately
> disappointed at the lack of ...stuff... They will fall into one of
> two categories: 1) the kind that will look through the journal,
> realize the stamp in the box may have something to do with the
> stamps in the journal, think that this may not exactly be a
> geocache, sign in pen or pencil and leave, or 2) the kind that will
> think the stamp is the ...stuff... they were looking for, take the
> stamp and leave some other ...stuff...
>
> It's the latter of these that concerns me. On more than one
> occasion, I have come to a letterbox with no stamp, but does contain
> a plastic alligator or wooden nickle. I then know that a category 2
> geocacher has been there. Because of this, I now write on the lid
> of all my boxes "This is not a geocache."
>
> Anyway, when I see ...stuff... in a letterbox I get worried that
> geocachers are thinking the letterbox is the geocache and it is only
> a matter of time until the stamp disappears and is replaced by Mardi
> Gras beads.
>
> So, what do you do with the ...stuff... take it out, leave it, or
> other?

jelybean_lb wrote:
Hello boys and girls out there in letterbox land. Got a question
for you. Today, team JeLyBean loaded up the candy wagon and headed
out for a 'boxing extravaganza. We found six today (why, thank you)
but four of the six had ...stuff... in them beyond the standard
stamp and logbook. By ...stuff... I mean the kind of things one
typically finds in a geocache.

(Short aside for those of you unfamiliar with geocaching: it's a lot
like letterboxing except you use GPS coordinates to find the box,
and instead of a stamp, the box is full of ...stuff... You bring
your own ...stuff... and take one of the pieces of ...stuff... out
of the geocache and leave one of your own pieces of ...stuff...
Other than that, it's very similar to letterboxing. Except we are
much more cool. Aside over.)

Anyway, here's the question. If you run into this kind
of ...stuff... what do you do with it? Here's the reason I'm
asking. In my experience, geocachers are not always familiar with
letterboxing and how we play our game. Owing to the relative
imprecision of GPS devices, geocachers often show up in an area and
begin hunting around for a likely spot to hide a geocache. They are
not aided by letterboxing clues like "look under the pile of rocks
at the base of the big tree." Because both letterboxers and
geocachers tend to place their boxes in cool areas, there is often a
letterbox and geocache near each other. Often, geocachers will find
the letterbox by mistake and think it is the geocache they were
searching for. The will then open the box and be immediately
disappointed at the lack of ...stuff... They will fall into one of
two categories: 1) the kind that will look through the journal,
realize the stamp in the box may have something to do with the
stamps in the journal, think that this may not exactly be a
geocache, sign in pen or pencil and leave, or 2) the kind that will
think the stamp is the ...stuff... they were looking for, take the
stamp and leave some other ...stuff...

It's the latter of these that concerns me. On more than one
occasion, I have come to a letterbox with no stamp, but does contain
a plastic alligator or wooden nickle. I then know that a category 2
geocacher has been there. Because of this, I now write on the lid
of all my boxes "This is not a geocache."

Anyway, when I see ...stuff... in a letterbox I get worried that
geocachers are thinking the letterbox is the geocache and it is only
a matter of time until the stamp disappears and is replaced by Mardi
Gras beads.

So, what do you do with the ...stuff... take it out, leave it, or
other?

'Box Happy... and don't take the stamp,
JeLyBean
